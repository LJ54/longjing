Subjective Social Capital and Loneliness for the Elderly: The Moderator Role of Line and Facebook Use (Work accepted to Social Media + Society)

The Problem

Social media has become a vital source of communication for older adults, but its impact is still an ongoing debate. This study investigated the moderating effect of Line and Facebook use on social capital and loneliness in older adults, aged 55 or more, using data obtained from the 2018 Taiwan Communication Survey (TCS).


Research methods

Secondary data analysis using binary logistic regression

Key Insights

  • Older adult users of Line have a lower possibility of being lonely than those who do not use Line.
  • Line could be a form of social media that has a positive effect on the relationship between social capital and loneliness.
  • Facebook does not have such a moderation effect.

    Profiling Instant Messaging App (Line) Users from the Uses and Gratification Theory Approach (Work under revision)


    The Problem

    It remained a problem for why people in Taiwan likes to use Line, an instant messaging (IM) app. The study explored the prioritized uses and gratifications motivations for using an, by classifying users by examining their demographic and personal characteristics.


    Research methods

    Secondary data analysis using latent class analysis to classify the users.


    Key Insights

  • Four kinds of users: non-users, relationship maintainers, all-purpose users, and Fear of Missing out (FoMo) users.
  • People who do not use the IM app were older and had lower bridging social capital. People who use the IM app were classified by different uses and gratifications and have a variety of levels of bridging and bonding social capital.
  • The FoMo users that specifically use the IM app to prevent them from missing their family and peers have considerably lower levels of loneliness and bridging social capital.
